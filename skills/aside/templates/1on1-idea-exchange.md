# 1:1 Idea Exchange

Use this template for conversations where ideas, framing, and specific language matter more than action items. This is the default template — it covers most 1:1 meetings where two people are exchanging perspectives, exploring a topic, or feeling out a relationship.

## Output Structure

```markdown
### [Title — a phrase that captures what was actually discussed, not a category]

**[Date] · [Participants] · [Duration if available]**

**Context:** [1-2 sentences on what led to this meeting and what each person came in wanting. If prep notes are available, draw from those.]

---

### What [other person] thinks

Capture the other person's worldview, frameworks, and opinions as expressed. Use their actual words in quotes when the phrasing is specific or memorable. Organize by theme, not chronology.

For each theme:
- **[Theme name drawn from their language]**: What they said, what they believe, how they frame it. Include direct quotes for distinctive phrasings. Note where they pushed back or disagreed.

### What emerged

Ideas, framings, or connections that neither person came in with — things that surfaced through the back-and-forth. These are the most valuable part of any conversation.

For each:
- **[Idea or framing]**: How it came up, who said what, why it matters. Use quotes. Cite vault connections with [[wikilinks]] where relevant.

### What I said that landed

Moments where something I said got a strong reaction, a follow-up question, or shifted the conversation. Brief — just enough to know what to develop further.

### Tensions and open threads

Places where there was disagreement, unresolved questions, or ideas that got raised but not explored. These are future starting points.

### Moves to make

Action items with their reasoning. Format: **[Action]** — [why, based on what was said]. Include names, companies, and specific context so these are useful weeks later.
```

## Rules

1. Every section header should use language from the actual conversation, not generic labels.
2. Include direct quotes for distinctive phrasings, analogies, and frameworks.
3. Distinguish speakers clearly when attributing.
4. Capture the other person's mental models and analogies in full.
5. Prioritize specificity over comprehensiveness.
6. For action items, always include the reasoning and conversational context.
7. If something was demonstrated or shown, describe what was shown and how the other person reacted.
8. Note energy shifts — moments where the conversation got more animated or where there was a pause.
9. Skip pleasantries and logistics unless they contained real content.
10. Reconstruct fragmented speech-to-text into intended meaning. Flag uncertain reconstructions.
