# Discovery Call

Use this template for client or prospect conversations where the goal is understanding their situation, identifying their needs, and determining fit. Useful for sales calls, consulting intake, partnership explorations, and "should we work together" conversations.

## Output Structure

```markdown
### [Title — what this call was really about, using their language]

**[Date] · [Participants] · [Duration if available]**

**Context:** [How this call came about. Who referred them, what they already knew coming in, what you wanted to learn.]

---

### Their background

Who they are and what they do, in enough detail to reconstruct their situation months later. Include company, role, scale, and anything that shapes how they see the world.

### Pain points and needs

What problems they described, in their words. Organize by theme. For each:
- **[Pain point in their language]**: What they said, how it affects their work, what they've tried. Direct quotes for anything specific.

Look for the gap between what they say they need and what they might actually need — note both without editorializing.

### Questions or concerns

Things they pushed back on, expressed uncertainty about, or wanted to know more about. These reveal what's between them and a yes.

### Budget and timeline

If discussed: what they're working with, when they need it, any constraints. If not discussed, note that explicitly — "Budget not discussed" is more useful than silence.

### Fit assessment

Your honest read on whether this is a good fit, based on the conversation. What aligns, what doesn't, what would need to be true. This section is for you, not for them.

### Next steps

Concrete actions with owners and deadlines where possible. Format: **[Action]** — [who does it, by when, why]. Include enough context to follow through without re-reading the whole note.
```

## Rules

1. Use their language for section content and theme names.
2. Separate what they said from your interpretation — keep "Fit assessment" as the only editorialized section.
3. Include direct quotes for how they describe their problems.
4. Note what wasn't said — topics you expected to come up but didn't.
5. If the transcript is from a group call (e.g., multiple people on their side), note who said what.
6. Capture any specific numbers, timelines, or constraints mentioned.
7. Reconstruct fragmented speech-to-text into intended meaning.
