# Group Conversation

Use this template for discussions with three or more participants where tracking who thinks what matters. Works for panel discussions, group brainstorms, team meetings with real debate, and dinner conversations worth remembering.

## Output Structure

```markdown
### [Title — the thread that held the conversation together]

**[Date] · [Participants] · [Duration if available]**

**Context:** [What brought this group together and what the nominal topic was. Note any relevant dynamics — who knows whom, what people came in with.]

---

### The through-line

The central question or theme that the conversation kept returning to, even when it wandered. State it as clearly as possible in 2-3 sentences. This may not match the official agenda.

### Perspectives

A section for each participant whose views were distinctive. Not everyone needs a section — only those who contributed a perspective worth tracking.

#### [Person name]
- Their core position or framing on the main topic
- Specific claims, analogies, or frameworks they introduced (with quotes)
- Where they pushed back on others
- What seemed to drive their perspective

[Repeat for each relevant participant]

### Collisions and combinations

Where perspectives clashed or combined into something new. These are the moments worth capturing:
- **[Collision or combination]**: Who said what, how it played off the other person's point, what emerged. Use quotes.

### Open threads

Questions raised but not resolved, ideas that got traction but weren't developed, tangents that might be worth returning to. Each with enough context to pick up later.

### My takeaways

What you personally took from this conversation. Where your thinking shifted, what you want to follow up on, what surprised you.

### Follow-ups

Concrete next steps. Format: **[Action]** — [who, why, context]. Include introductions to make, ideas to explore, and conversations to continue.
```

## Rules

1. Track perspectives per person — don't flatten a group into consensus if there wasn't one.
2. Use direct quotes for distinctive phrasings, especially when people disagreed.
3. "The through-line" is the real topic, which may differ from the stated agenda.
4. "Collisions and combinations" is the most valuable section — prioritize moments where ideas interacted.
5. Not every participant needs equal coverage. Focus on those who shaped the conversation.
6. If the transcript has poor speaker attribution, do your best to distinguish voices from context and note where attribution is uncertain.
7. Reconstruct fragmented speech-to-text into intended meaning.
